It's Pronounced "Aleh-HAHN-dra"

On the first day of school, Ms. Peterson stood at the front of the classroom holding a clipboard while students chatted nervously at their desks. “Okay everyone,” she said cheerfully, “I’m going to take attendance.”
One by one, she called names.
“Emily?” “Here.” “Jason?” “Present.” Then she paused. “Uh…” She squinted down at the paper. “Ale… Alex… Alejandria?”
A few kids snickered quietly.
In the back row, Alejandra felt her cheeks grow warm.
“It’s pronounced Aleh-HAHN-dra,” she corrected softly.
Ms. Peterson smiled awkwardly. “Right, sorry about that.”
Alejandra nodded quickly, but the embarrassment stayed with her for the rest of class.
It wasn’t the first time it had happened.
Teachers shortened her name constantly or avoided saying it altogether. Sometimes people asked if they could “just call her Alex” because it was easier for them. Even at tennis practice, her coach sometimes looked nervous during attendance and paused dramatically before saying her name wrong anyway. At lunch, her friend Priya sat beside her.
“You okay?” she asked. Alejandra poked at her sandwich. “Yeah.”
Priya raised an eyebrow. “You got quiet.”
Alejandra sighed. “I just don’t get why people never even try to say my name correctly.”
Priya nodded immediately. “I know exactly what you mean.”
“How do you deal with it?”
Priya shrugged. “Honestly? I stopped pretending it doesn’t bother me.”
Alejandra thought about that the rest of the day.
The next morning during attendance, Ms. Peterson reached Alejandra’s name again and hesitated.
Before she could guess, Alejandra smiled politely.
“It’s Aleh-HAHN-dra,” she said clearly. “You can sound it out like this if it helps.”
To her surprise, Ms. Peterson grabbed a pen immediately and wrote little pronunciation notes beside the name.
“Oh! Thank you,” she said genuinely. “I want to make sure I say it correctly.”
This time, her voice sounded different.
Not annoyed. Not embarrassed.
Just willing to learn. Later that week, Ms. Peterson got it perfectly right during class.
“Aleh-HAHN-dra, would you like to read the next paragraph?”
A small smile spread across Alejandra’s face.
After class, Ms. Peterson stopped her near the door.
“Thank you for helping me learn your name properly,” she said kindly. “Names are important.”
Alejandra nodded.
At home that evening, her grandmother smiled when Alejandra told her the story.
“You know why your parents chose your name?” her grandmother asked.
Alejandra shook her head.
“Because it belonged to your great-grandmother,” she explained. “She was strong, smart, and brave. Your name carries history.” Alejandra thought about that quietly.
For so long, hearing her name mispronounced had made her feel small, like part of her identity was inconvenient.
But now she realized something important.
Her name was not “too hard.”
It was meaningful.
And it deserved to be said correctly.
